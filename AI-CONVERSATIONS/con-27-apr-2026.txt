I said:
I'm starting my Smart Finance Assistant project. I need help defining the problem clearly. What finance problems could a CSV-based tool solve for students and young adults?

Claude said:
Here are five specific, realistic problems a CSV-based finance tool could address for students:

1. **Not knowing where money goes**: Most students have bank exports but the raw list of transactions is hard to interpret. A tool that groups by category and shows totals solves this immediately.

2. **Refunds being counted as spending**: When a refund appears in the CSV as a negative amount, naive totals will undercount spending. Properly separating refunds from real spending gives a more accurate picture.

3. **No comparison to a budget**: Students often have a rough weekly budget in their head but no easy way to compare actual spending against it. A budget comparison with a clear "over/under" result is immediately useful.

4. **Identifying the highest-cost category**: Most people don't realise how much they spend on coffee or entertainment until they see it summed up. Showing the top spending category creates actionable awareness.

5. **Not knowing how long to save for a goal**: "How long until I can afford a new laptop?" is a common question. A simple savings goal calculator answers this in seconds.

For Budget Buddy, I'd focus on problems 1, 2, 3, and 5 — they cover the full journey from raw data to actionable insight.

**Inputs:**

- Highest spending category
- Budget comparison result (over/under/no budget set)
- Plain-English financial insights and recommendations
- Chatbot responses to user questions
- Savings goal timeline
- Gradio web interface that ties everything together

The clean separation between inputs and outputs helps you design your functions — each function should take the inputs it needs and return the outputs clearly. This is the "Identify Inputs and Outputs" step in the six-step methodology.
